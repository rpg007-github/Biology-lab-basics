01Scientific Method

The scientific method is the systematic process scientists use to investigate the natural world. Every experiment starts with an observation, moves to a testable hypothesis, and ends with data that either supports or fails to support the hypothesis. You never "prove" a hypothesis in science; you support it or reject it.

Steps of the Scientific Method

The process flows from observation to question to hypothesis to experiment to data analysis to conclusion. A good hypothesis is testable, falsifiable, and written as an if/then statement. The purpose of an experiment is to test a hypothesis under controlled conditions so that cause-and-effect relationships can be identified.

Theory vs. hypothesis: A hypothesis is a testable prediction. A theory is a well-tested, widely accepted explanation supported by extensive evidence (e.g., cell theory, theory of evolution). A theory is not a guess.

Microscope Care & Use

Always carry with two hands (one on the arm, one under the base). Start on the lowest objective when focusing a new slide. Use the coarse adjustment knob only on scanning and low power. On high power, use fine adjustment only to avoid cracking the slide. Clean lenses only with lens paper. Store with the lowest objective clicked in place and the stage lowered.

Making a Wet Mount

Place the specimen in a drop of water on a clean slide. Lower a coverslip at an angle (about 45 degrees) to one edge of the water drop, then slowly release it to minimize air bubbles. The water spreads between the slide and coverslip, keeping the specimen flat, hydrated, and transparent enough for light to pass through.

Remember
Microscope images are inverted and reversed. To move the image right, move the slide left. To move it up, move the slide down. Move the slide in the same direction the image appears to center it.

04Biological Molecules & Nutrition

There are four major classes of biological macromolecules. Each is built from smaller monomers joined by dehydration synthesis (removes water) and broken apart by hydrolysis (adds water). You also need to know key nutritional terms like vitamins, minerals, and calories.

The Four Macromolecules

MacromoleculeMonomerElementsFunctionsLab Test
CarbohydratesMonosaccharidesC, H, OQuick energy, structureBenedict's (sugars), Iodine (starch)
LipidsGlycerol + fatty acidsC, H, OLong-term energy, membranes, insulationBrown paper bag / Sudan IV
ProteinsAmino acidsC, H, O, N, (S)Enzymes, structure, transport, defenseBiuret (turns purple)
Nucleic acidsNucleotidesC, H, O, N, PGenetic information (DNA, RNA)Dische diphenylamine
Lab Test Results to Memorize
Benedict's: blue (neg) → orange/red (pos for simple sugars; must heat). Iodine: yellow-brown (neg) → blue-black (pos for starch). Biuret: blue (neg) → purple/violet (pos for protein). Sudan IV / paper bag: translucent spot or red-stained (pos for lipids).

Carbohydrate Details

Monosaccharides (simple sugars) include glucose, fructose, and galactose. Disaccharides form when two monosaccharides bond: sucrose (glucose + fructose), lactose (glucose + galactose), maltose (glucose + glucose). Polysaccharides include starch (plant energy storage), glycogen (animal energy storage), cellulose (plant cell wall structure), and chitin (arthropod exoskeletons, fungal cell walls).

Lipid Details

A triglyceride is one glycerol bonded to three fatty acids. Saturated fats have no double bonds between carbons, pack tightly, and are solid at room temperature (butter, animal fat). Unsaturated fats have one or more double bonds, creating kinks, and are liquid at room temperature (olive oil). Phospholipids have a hydrophilic head and two hydrophobic tails, making them ideal for cell membranes.

Protein Structure

Twenty amino acids are used in living things, linked by peptide bonds. Protein structure has four levels: primary (amino acid sequence), secondary (alpha helices and beta pleated sheets from hydrogen bonding), tertiary (3D folding from R-group interactions), and quaternary (multiple polypeptide subunits). Denaturing a protein destroys its shape but not its peptide bonds; caused by extreme heat, pH, or heavy metals.

Bond Reactions
Dehydration synthesis: monomer + monomer → polymer + H₂O
Hydrolysis: polymer + H₂O → monomer + monomer

Nutritional Terms

TermDefinition
Calorie (kcal)Unit of energy. One dietary Calorie (capital C) = 1 kilocalorie = energy to raise 1 kg of water by 1°C. Carbs and proteins provide ~4 kcal/g; fats provide ~9 kcal/g.
VitaminOrganic molecule needed in small amounts for metabolism. The body cannot synthesize most vitamins, so they must come from the diet. Examples: vitamin C (collagen synthesis), vitamin D (calcium absorption).
MineralInorganic element required for body functions. Examples: calcium (bones, muscle contraction), iron (hemoglobin), potassium (nerve impulses). Unlike vitamins, minerals are elements, not complex molecules.

05Cell Structure & Function

Cell theory states that all living things are made of cells, the cell is the basic unit of life, and all cells come from pre-existing cells. You need to know the differences between prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells, between plant and animal cells, and the function of every major organelle.

Cyanobacteria are prokaryotes that perform photosynthesis. They are sometimes called blue-green algae but are bacteria, not true algae. They have no chloroplasts; instead, their photosynthetic membranes (thylakoids) are in the cytoplasm. They were likely the first organisms to produce oxygen on Earth.

Cytoplasmic streaming is the directed flow of cytoplasm within a cell, visible in plant cells like Elodea. It moves organelles, nutrients, and proteins through the cell using the cytoskeleton (actin microfilaments and motor proteins). Endosymbiotic Theory
Mitochondria and chloroplasts were once free-living prokaryotes engulfed by ancestral eukaryotic cells. Evidence: both have their own circular DNA, 70S ribosomes, double membranes, and divide by binary fission.

07Enzymes

An enzyme is a biological catalyst, almost always a protein, that speeds up chemical reactions without being consumed. Enzymes lower the activation energy needed for a reaction to proceed. Each enzyme is specific to its substrate because of the shape of its active site. The substrate binds the active site to form an enzyme-substrate complex, which then releases product. The enzyme is recycled and reused.

TermDefinition
CatalystA substance that speeds up a reaction without being used up
Activation energyMinimum energy needed to start a reaction; enzymes lower this
SubstrateThe molecule an enzyme acts on
Active siteRegion of the enzyme where the substrate binds
Enzyme-substrate complexTemporary structure formed when substrate binds the active site
ProductWhat is produced after the enzyme catalyzes the reaction
Enzyme specificityEach enzyme fits only one substrate (or a small group), like a lock and key
DenaturationLoss of 3D shape; the enzyme can no longer bind substrate (usually irreversible)
Enzyme Reaction
E + S → ES complex → E + P
Enzyme is released unchanged and can catalyze the reaction again.

Lab connection: The catalase lab uses liver (source of catalase) and H₂O₂ (substrate). Catalase breaks H₂O₂ into water and O₂ gas (the bubbles). Boiled liver shows no reaction because the enzyme is denatured. Changing pH or temperature alters the rate.

08Cell Cycle & Cell Division

The cell cycle describes the life of a cell from one division to the next. It includes interphase (G1, S, G2) and the mitotic phase (mitosis + cytokinesis). Interphase accounts for roughly 90% of the cell cycle. DNA replication occurs during the S (synthesis) phase.

Chromosome Numbers

A diploid cell (2n) has two copies of each chromosome (one from each parent). Humans: 2n = 46. After mitosis, each daughter cell is still diploid (2n = 46). After meiosis I, cells are haploid (n = 23). After meiosis II, four haploid gametes exist. During mitosis, the chromosome number does not change. During meiosis, it is halved.

Mendel's Laws

The Law of Segregation states that two alleles for each gene separate during gamete formation, so each gamete carries only one allele. The Law of Independent Assortment states that genes on different chromosomes sort independently during meiosis.

Probability & Chi-Square

Probability is used to predict genetic outcomes. The probability of two independent events both occurring is the product of their individual probabilities (multiplication rule).

Chi-Square Test
χ² = Σ (observed - expected)² / expected

Degrees of freedom = number of categories - 1. Compare to critical value at p = 0.05. If χ² exceeds the critical value, reject the null hypothesis (observed and expected are significantly different).

10DNA, RNA & Molecular Biology

DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) stores genetic information. RNA (ribonucleic acid) helps read and carry out those instructions. You need to know the structure of both, how they differ, the base-pairing rules, and the central dogma: DNA → RNA → Protein.

Nucleotide Structure

A nucleotide is the monomer of nucleic acids. Each has three parts: a phosphate group, a five-carbon sugar (deoxyribose in DNA, ribose in RNA), and a nitrogenous base. Purines (adenine, guanine) have two rings. Pyrimidines (thymine, cytosine, uracil) have one ring.

Base Pairing Rules
DNA: A-T (2 H-bonds), G-C (3 H-bonds). RNA: A-U, G-C. "Apples in the Tree, Car in the Garage." In RNA, uracil replaces thymine.

The Genetic Code

A codon is a three-base sequence on mRNA that codes for one amino acid. An anticodon is the complementary three-base sequence on tRNA. The start codon AUG codes for methionine and signals the beginning of translation. The stop codons (UAA, UAG, UGA) signal the ribosome to release the polypeptide. The genetic code is universal (nearly the same in all organisms), redundant (multiple codons can code for the same amino acid), and non-overlapping (codons are read sequentially without sharing bases).

A gene is a segment of DNA that contains the instructions for making one protein (or functional RNA). A peptide bond links amino acids together during translation.

11DNA Technology & Recombination

Modern molecular biology labs involve manipulating DNA in ways that go beyond basic replication and transcription. You need to understand how DNA recombination occurs naturally, how bacteria exchange genes, and the tools scientists use to study and manipulate DNA.

DNA Recombination & Bacterial Conjugation

ProcessDescription
Genetic recombinationRearrangement of DNA sequences to produce new combinations of genes. In eukaryotes, crossing over during meiosis is the main mechanism.
Bacterial conjugationTransfer of genetic material (usually a plasmid) from one bacterium to another through a pilus (a direct cell-to-cell bridge). This is horizontal gene transfer and a major way antibiotic resistance spreads.
TransformationA bacterium picks up free DNA from the environment and incorporates it into its genome.
TransductionA bacteriophage (virus) accidentally carries bacterial DNA from one cell to another.

Key Tools of DNA Technology

Tool / ConceptDefinition
PlasmidSmall, circular DNA molecule in bacteria, separate from chromosomal DNA. Can replicate independently. Used as a vector in genetic engineering.
VectorA vehicle (usually a plasmid or virus) used to carry foreign DNA into a host cell for cloning or expression.
Restriction enzymeEnzyme that cuts DNA at specific recognition sequences. Creates fragments that can be separated or recombined.
Gel electrophoresisTechnique that separates DNA fragments by size. Smaller fragments travel farther. DNA migrates toward the positive electrode because of its negatively charged phosphate backbone.

Gel electrophoresis then separates extracted or digested DNA fragments by size.

12Evolution & Natural Selection

Evolution is the change in allele frequencies in a population over time. Natural selection is the primary mechanism: individuals with traits better suited to the environment survive and reproduce at higher rates, passing those traits on.

Key Definitions

TermDefinition
EvolutionChange in allele frequencies in a population over generations
Natural selectionDifferential survival and reproduction based on heritable traits
PopulationGroup of individuals of the same species living in the same area
SpeciesGroup of organisms that can interbreed and produce fertile offspring
FossilPreserved remains or traces of organisms from the past; provides evidence for evolution

How Natural Selection Works

Four conditions are required: variation exists within the population, some of that variation is heritable, more offspring are produced than can survive (overproduction), and individuals with advantageous traits survive and reproduce more (differential reproduction). Over generations, allele frequencies shift and the advantageous traits become more common.

Natural selection acts on phenotypes, not genotypes. The environment determines which phenotypes are favored. Over time, this changes the genetic makeup of the population. Natural selection does not act on individuals; individuals do not evolve. Populations evolve.

Evidence for Evolution

Evidence TypeExplanation
Fossil recordShows change over time and transitional forms
Comparative anatomyHomologous structures (same structure, different function) indicate common ancestry
EmbryologySimilar early development across species (e.g., vertebrate gill slits)
Molecular biologyDNA and protein sequence similarities correlate with evolutionary relatedness
BiogeographyGeographic distribution of species matches evolutionary predictions
